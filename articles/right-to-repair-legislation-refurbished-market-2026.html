Right to Repair in 2026: How New Legislation Is Reshaping the Refurbished Electronics Market

Standard Mobile Company ResearchApril 18, 20269 min read

For years, independent repair shops and refurbishers operated in a gray zone—sourcing parts from overseas suppliers, reverse-engineering diagnostic software, and hoping OEMs wouldn't lock them out with the next firmware update. That era is ending. A wave of right-to-repair legislation across the United States and Europe is forcing manufacturers to open their parts catalogs, share repair manuals, and provide the same diagnostic tools they offer authorized service providers. The implications for the $78 billion global refurbished electronics market are profound.

The Legislative Landscape: Where We Stand

Right-to-repair advocacy has been building momentum for over a decade, but 2024–2026 marked the tipping point. Following New York's landmark Digital Fair Repair Act in 2023, a cascade of states passed their own versions. California's SB 244, which took effect in July 2024, set the template with its requirement that manufacturers of devices priced above $50 must provide parts, tools, and documentation for at least seven years after the last unit is sold. By early 2026, 32 states have enacted some form of electronics repair legislation.

Oregon's law is particularly significant for refurbishers because it directly addresses parts pairing—the practice of serializing components so that a replacement screen or battery triggers a software warning or disables features unless installed by an authorized technician. Under SB 1596, manufacturers cannot use software locks to prevent a functionally identical OEM part from working as intended.

What the Laws Actually Require

While specifics vary, most right-to-repair statutes share three core mandates that directly affect refurbishment operations:

1. OEM parts availability

Manufacturers must sell genuine replacement parts to independent repair providers and consumers at “fair and reasonable” prices. This is a seismic shift. Previously, obtaining an authentic Apple display assembly or a Samsung motherboard outside authorized channels meant navigating a patchwork of aftermarket suppliers with inconsistent quality. Under the new laws, refurbishers can order directly from OEMs or their designated parts distributors, receiving the same components used in authorized repair centers.

2. Diagnostic tools and software

OEMs must provide access to the same diagnostic and calibration software used by their own technicians. For smartphones, this means tools to recalibrate True Tone displays, pair new batteries with battery health reporting, and run post-repair system diagnostics. Apple's Self Service Repair program, initially limited in scope, has expanded significantly under regulatory pressure to include full calibration capabilities for independent shops.

3. Repair manuals and schematics

Service documentation—including step-by-step repair procedures, board-level schematics, and troubleshooting guides—must be made available. Samsung's Repair Mode and Apple's expanded repair manuals now cover procedures that were previously restricted to Genius Bar technicians and Apple Authorized Service Providers.

“For the first time, independent refurbishers have a legal right to the same parts and information that authorized repair centers use. That levels a playing field that's been tilted for two decades.”
— Gay Gordon-Byrne, Executive Director, Repair.org

Impact on Refurbishment Costs and Economics

Access to OEM parts and tools is reshaping the cost structure of refurbishment in measurable ways. Our analysis of industry data from Q1 2026 shows several clear trends.

Parts cost reduction

Counterintuitively, OEM parts obtained through official channels are often less expensive than the high-quality aftermarket equivalents refurbishers previously relied on. A genuine iPhone 15 display assembly through Apple's parts store costs $199, compared to $220–$260 for a premium aftermarket OLED panel that still couldn't pass software validation. When you factor in the elimination of returns due to parts-pairing failures, effective per-unit parts costs for many refurbishers have dropped 12–18%.

Reduced failure rates

Genuine parts combined with proper calibration tools have cut post-repair failure rates significantly. Refurbishers report that warranty claim rates on devices repaired with OEM components and official diagnostics are running at 2.1%, compared to 6.8% with aftermarket parts—a 69% reduction that directly improves unit economics.

Throughput and grading improvements

Access to manufacturer diagnostic suites means refurbishers can run the same 60+ point functional tests that OEMs use during production. This enables more precise cosmetic and functional grading, which in turn supports higher resale prices for top-tier units. Devices that can be certified as passing OEM-equivalent testing command a 9–14% premium on wholesale markets compared to those tested with generic tools.

How Apple and Samsung Are Responding

Apple's evolving strategy

Apple's relationship with right-to-repair has been a slow-motion pivot. After years of lobbying against repair legislation, Apple reversed course in late 2023 and began supporting California's SB 244. By 2026, Apple's Self Service Repair program covers iPhone 14 through iPhone 17, all M-series MacBooks, and iPad Pro models. The company now sells over 300 individual parts through its online store and provides a full-featured version of Apple Diagnostics to any registered independent repair provider.

However, Apple's compliance has been selective. The company still restricts board-level repair schematics for its newest devices and charges licensing fees for its calibration software that some smaller shops find prohibitive—$49 per month for the basic tier, $199 for full access including board-level diagnostics. Repair advocates argue this creates a two-tier system that technically satisfies the law while still advantaging larger operations.

Samsung's open repair initiative

Samsung has taken a more aggressive approach, partnering with iFixit since 2022 and steadily expanding its parts catalog. As of Q1 2026, Samsung provides parts for Galaxy S-series phones dating back to the S21, plus its entire Galaxy Tab and Galaxy Book lineup. Samsung's diagnostic app, available to any registered repair provider at no cost, supports full component verification and calibration—a notable contrast to Apple's paid model.

Impact on Refurbished Device Pricing

The expanded parts access is having a measurable effect on refurbished device pricing across the market. Better parts availability increases the supply of high-grade refurbished units, which puts downward pressure on prices at the top end. At the same time, devices that were previously uneconomical to repair—those needing expensive or hard-to-source components—are now entering the refurbished pipeline, expanding overall supply.

Our tracking data shows that average refurbished smartphone prices dropped 6.3% year-over-year in Q1 2026, but margins for refurbishers actually improved by 2.1 percentage points because input costs fell faster than selling prices. The economics are particularly favorable for devices in the 2–3 year old category, where parts are now readily available but consumer demand remains strong.

The EU dimension

The European Union's Right to Repair Directive, entering its phased implementation in 2026, goes further than any U.S. state law. It mandates repairability scoring on product labels (similar to energy efficiency ratings), requires manufacturers to design products for easier disassembly, and extends spare parts obligations to 10 years for large appliances and 5 years for consumer electronics. For refurbishers operating in or exporting to European markets, compliance with these standards is becoming a competitive differentiator.

Challenges and Limitations

Despite the progress, significant gaps remain. Most state laws exempt products sold before their effective dates, leaving a large installed base of older devices still locked behind proprietary repair barriers. Enforcement mechanisms vary widely—some states have dedicated enforcement through their attorney general's office, while others rely on private action, which is expensive and slow.

  • Pricing ambiguity: “Fair and reasonable” pricing is not precisely defined in most statutes, giving OEMs room to set parts prices that technically comply while remaining uncompetitive.
  • Software locks persist: While Oregon banned parts pairing, many states have not, and manufacturers continue to use software validation on newer devices in those jurisdictions.
  • Security exemptions: OEMs can withhold certain tools and documentation on security grounds, a carve-out that Apple has used to limit access to biometric sensor calibration.
  • Federal preemption risk: A federal right-to-repair bill remains stalled in committee, and some industry groups are pushing for a weaker federal standard that would preempt stronger state laws.

What This Means for the Market Going Forward

The right-to-repair movement has crossed the threshold from advocacy to infrastructure. For refurbishers and secondary market operators, the practical implications are clear: parts sourcing is becoming more predictable, repair quality is improving, and the economics of refurbishment are tilting in favor of operations that invest in proper tooling and certification.

We expect three developments over the next 12–18 months. First, the remaining holdout states will likely pass their own legislation, pushed by constituent demand and the precedent set by early adopters. Second, OEM parts pricing will continue to compress as competition from multiple authorized distributors increases supply. Third, the quality gap between “authorized” and “independent” refurbished devices will narrow, which will accelerate consumer adoption of refurbished products across all price tiers.

For businesses in the refurbished electronics supply chain, now is the time to formalize OEM parts relationships, invest in manufacturer-provided diagnostic tools, and update quality processes to leverage the new access these laws provide. The companies that move fastest will capture the margin advantage before it becomes table stakes.
